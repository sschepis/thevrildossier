## The Masonic Evidence

The ritual initiation interpretation rests on specific, verifiable documentary evidence that merits detailed examination.

### The Legend of Hiram Abiff

The central drama of the Third Degree (Master Mason) is the legend of Hiram Abiff, presented as the chief architect of King Solomon's Temple. Hiram alone possesses the secret Master's Word—the knowledge that confers authority and power. Three Fellow Craft Masons (the "three ruffians," named Jubela, Jubelo, and Jubelum) conspire to extract this secret by force.

The legend unfolds at the three gates of the Temple:

1. **At the south gate**: Jubela accosts Hiram and demands the Master's secrets. When Hiram refuses, Jubela strikes him across the **right temple** with a twenty-four-inch gauge (or plumb rule, depending on the jurisdiction).

2. **At the west gate**: Jubelo confronts Hiram with the same demand. Upon refusal, he strikes Hiram across the **left temple** with a square (or level).

3. **At the east gate**: Jubelum delivers the final blow—a strike to the **forehead** with a setting maul (a heavy mallet). This is the killing blow. Hiram falls dead.

The candidate, playing Hiram, is symbolically killed—laid in a "grave" (often a canvas on the lodge floor). The Worshipful Master then raises the candidate using the **Strong Grip of a Master Mason** (the Lion's Paw), symbolically resurrecting him into a new identity as a Master Mason.

### Duncan's Masonic Ritual and Monitor (1866)

The primary source for this ritual is Malcolm C. Duncan's *Masonic Ritual and Monitor*, published in 1866 and never successfully suppressed despite Masonic objections to its publication. Duncan's work provides verbatim ritual scripts, including the oaths, the physical actions of each degree, and the symbolic explanations provided to candidates. Key elements documented by Duncan:

- The "three grand Masonic blows" targeting right temple, left temple, and forehead
- The candidate's symbolic death and burial
- The raising by the Worshipful Master's grip
- The Entered Apprentice oath: "...binding myself under no less penalty than to have my throat cut across, my tongue torn out by its roots, and my body buried in the rough sands of the sea..."
- The progressive revelation structure: each degree reveals more, but the candidate is never told the full scope of what lies above

Multiple independent sources across two centuries—including Samuel Prichard's *Masonry Dissected* (1730), William Morgan's *Illustrations of Masonry* (1826), and Léo Taxil's various exposés—confirm the essential structure of the ritual, despite variations in detail across jurisdictions.
