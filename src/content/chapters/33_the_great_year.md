## The Heliospheric Current Sheet

The Heliospheric Current Sheet (HCS) is the boundary surface within the solar system where the Sun's magnetic field reverses polarity — the dividing line between the Sun's magnetic north and south hemispheres. It extends from the Sun to the edge of the heliosphere, undulating like a spinning ballerina's skirt as it rotates with the Sun.

In September 2025, a paper published in *Frontiers in Astronomy and Space Sciences* by Du et al. (Boston University / Los Alamos National Laboratory) analyzed heliospheric current sheet crossings detected by both the Parker Solar Probe (0.07–0.3 AU from the Sun) and the Advanced Composition Explorer (0.99 AU — near Earth). - **Nearly all crossings showed signatures of magnetic reconnection**: 7 of 8 at Parker Solar Probe's close range; 5 of 8 at Earth's distance
- Reconnection occurs across a variety of solar wind speeds and plasma conditions
- The aspect ratio (width/length) of reconnecting current sheets tends to be less than 0.01 — extremely thin, knife-like structures

This means the heliospheric current sheet is not a passive boundary. It is **actively reconnecting** — releasing energy throughout the solar system at a rate that appears to be increasing with solar activity.
